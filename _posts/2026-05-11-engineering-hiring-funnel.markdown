We hired 18 engineers in one year from a pool of over 1,000 candidates. That's a 1.6% application-to-hire rate.

That number has a counterpart: 89% of the offers we extended that year got accepted. The two statistics point to the same thing — a process that filters carefully enough to find the right people and engages them seriously enough that they want the job by the time they get the offer.

## What the funnel actually looks like

From roughly 1,100 initial applications:
- About 370 passed initial screening and entered active review
- About 100 reached a substantive interview stage
- 18 received offers
- Of those 18, 16 accepted

That's the shape of it. A wide initial funnel, significant drop-off at each stage, and a high close rate at the end.

By the time someone gets an offer, they've spent real time with the team and self-selected in. Offer acceptance isn't a mystery — it's what happens when you don't advance people before there's real mutual investment. Surprises at the offer stage are mostly a symptom of a process that moved too fast.

## Why selectivity isn't about ego

"Selective hiring" sounds like it might be about status signaling. It isn't. The cost of a hiring mistake in engineering is substantial and asymmetric. A bad hire on a ten-person team affects every engineer they work with. They slow code review, introduce friction in design conversations, and create rework. They're also expensive to address — the cycle of trying to improve the situation, realizing it's not working, and navigating an exit takes six months minimum.

Leaving a role open for another four weeks costs less than spending three months managing a bad hire out.

In practice this breaks down under timeline pressure. When you're behind on delivery and the engineering lead is asking when the headcount is coming, extending a mediocre offer feels like progress. The teams I've seen get into hiring trouble almost always cite timeline pressure as the driver. We made this hire faster than we should have because we needed someone.

The selectivity at 1.6% isn't a target — it's what happens when you hold the bar consistently across cycles, including under pressure.

## What we're actually evaluating

The bar has shifted over time as the nature of the work has changed.

A few years ago, evaluation was heavily weighted toward execution: can you build this thing, in this time, with these constraints. That's still relevant but it's no longer sufficient. As AI tools become central to how engineers work, what differentiates strong engineers has shifted toward judgment: can you specify a problem clearly enough for a system to work on it, can you critically evaluate AI-generated output, can you architect solutions at a level above implementation?

An engineer who can write code but can't articulate what the code should do — clearly enough that a less technical stakeholder understands it, or clearly enough that an AI model can implement it correctly — is at a disadvantage in how teams work now. We've adjusted evaluation accordingly.

## The cultural component isn't separate from the technical

We don't treat culture fit as a soft add-on checked at the end of the process. It's woven throughout.

The reason: technical skill doesn't predict how someone will behave when they disagree with a decision, when they find a mistake in someone else's work, or when they're asked to do something they think is wrong. Those behaviors matter enormously in a close-knit engineering team, and they're much harder to change after hire than technical gaps.

The specific things we look for are extensions of the culture principles I've written about elsewhere — how someone handles being wrong, whether they ask for help or try to disappear a problem, how they talk about previous employers or teammates. You can surface all of this in a well-structured conversation. Most interviewers aren't listening for it.

## What happens after the offer

We track onboarding success at 90%. That means nine in ten new hires reach the point where they're independently productive without requiring exceptional management support to get there.

That number is a function of both hiring quality and onboarding quality. Hiring the right people and then doing a bad job of integrating them still produces poor outcomes. We've invested in structured onboarding — clear first-week expectations, an explicit integration path, and regular check-ins with enough frequency to catch problems before they compound.

The onboarding success rate matters because it's the leading indicator of whether new hires will still be there in a year. Engineers who struggle in the first 90 days either leave or disengage. Getting them to independent productivity quickly is the intervention that prevents both.

## Engineering turnover as a hiring metric

We report engineering turnover at approximately 6%. For context, technology sector voluntary turnover typically runs well above that.

I track this because it's the real measure of hiring quality. If you hire well — people who are genuinely suited to the work and the environment — they stay. If you hire to fill slots and optimize for time-to-hire, you get higher turnover and a perpetually leaky funnel that requires constant top-of-funnel activity to compensate.

A low turnover team requires less hiring. That seems obvious but it's often not factored into the cost-benefit of being more selective. Spending three extra weeks finding the right candidate saves far more than three weeks of management attention if the alternative is managing out a bad hire six months later.

## Holding the bar under pressure

When the team is stretched and a position has been open for two months, the pressure to move faster is real. A few things that help:

Being explicit with the team about what timeline you're willing to tolerate — and why — takes pressure off the interviewers. When people know the bar is being held deliberately, they stop advocating for faster movement on borderline candidates.

Using the open role as a forcing function to understand what you actually need is more useful than most teams expect. "We need a backend engineer" often conceals three different unstated requirements that would produce very different hires. The selectivity process forces that clarity early.

And accepting that some positions will take longer than you want is part of running a high-bar process. The right candidate pool is genuinely thinner for some roles. The alternative — moving faster to close the position — produces results that are consistent but predictably worse.

The 89% offer acceptance rate isn't an accident. It reflects a process where candidates who reach the offer stage have had enough real exposure to the team and the work that they already know whether they want to be there.
