Why I do this

From banking to breach prevention

I started writing software for banks. The work was unglamorous — core systems, transaction logic, nothing that ended up on TechCrunch. But it taught me something that stuck: when data fails, real people feel it immediately. A frozen account, a delayed transfer, a lost record. That weight changed how I think about software.

When I moved into cloud security, the stakes felt familiar. The system either holds or it doesn't, and people on the other end always find out. I stopped treating security as a separate discipline and started seeing it as the baseline expectation for anything I ship.

What I care about in leadership

The best engineers I've worked with weren't the fastest — they were the ones who asked why before writing a line. I try to hire for that instinct and protect it once it's inside a team. Good architecture is mostly good thinking shared clearly, and my job is to create conditions where that thinking can happen: small teams, real ownership, context before process.

What I push back on

Security theater. The industry has a habit of measuring effort — dashboards added, certifications collected, alerts acknowledged — while actual attack surfaces keep growing. I've built security products from the inside and I know the difference between features that look impressive in a demo and ones that actually stop a breach at 2am. I care about the second kind.
