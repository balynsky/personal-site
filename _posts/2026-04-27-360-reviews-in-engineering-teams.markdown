A performance review built around individual output gives you roughly half the picture. The manager can see what was delivered, how clearly someone communicates upward, whether goals were met. What that view misses is everything that happens sideways — across the team, in code reviews, in the conversations where someone is either unblocking people or creating friction.

We run annual performance reviews with 360-degree peer input. I've run this process long enough to have a clear view of what it changes and what it doesn't.

## What top-down reviews miss

A manager evaluates what they can observe. For individual contributors, that's usually: did they complete their work, did they communicate clearly upward, did they solve technical problems assigned to them.

What's harder to see from the management layer: how often does this person unblock their teammates? Do other engineers go to them when they're stuck, or treat them as someone to route around? Whether they share what they know or hold it close doesn't show up in any deliverable.

These things matter enormously for team performance. An engineer who is individually excellent but creates friction for everyone around them can cost the team more than they contribute. An engineer who isn't the strongest individual contributor but has become the person everyone turns to when they're confused might be worth more to the team's throughput than their output metrics suggest.

Top-down reviews systematically miss this. Managers can't see what they're not in the room for.

## What 360 input adds

When the review process includes structured input from peers, two things change.

First, the signal set expands. You now have data on how the person operates across relationships, not just on deliverables. This surfaces both problems and contributions that wouldn't otherwise be visible.

Second — and this is the part most people underestimate — it changes how people think about their own role. If you know your performance review includes input from the people you work alongside every day, you naturally pay more attention to those relationships. The implicit signal of "this relationship counts" shapes behavior over time.

360 reviews are only as useful as the quality of the input they gather and how seriously people give and take feedback.

## The honesty problem

The most common failure mode in 360 reviews is feedback that's too positive to be useful. Peers soften criticism, especially in cultures where conflict is uncomfortable. You end up with reviews that say "great communicator, could work on X" where X is so vaguely described it couldn't possibly drive behavior change.

The solution isn't to force negative feedback — it's to create the conditions where honest feedback feels safe. That means reviewing without punishment for the reviewer. It means managers treating 360 input as information to be understood, not accusations to be adjudicated. And it means, over time, demonstrating that feedback actually affects decisions.

On our team, the norm is that mistakes don't get penalized — they get examined. That principle extends to reviews. If someone's 360 surfaces a pattern that needs addressing, the response is a real conversation, not a demotion or a performance plan. People learn this over time, and the honesty of the feedback improves.

## Engineering turnover as a lagging indicator

We track engineering turnover carefully. Ours has consistently run well below industry norms — at roughly 6%, compared to a technology sector average that often sits above 20%.

The review process is one piece of it — culture, compensation, and management quality all contribute. But a review system that surfaces the contributions people actually care about making does affect whether they stay.

When engineers feel that their growth and their impact on the team are being seen, they're less likely to go looking for an environment where they will be. When they trust that the feedback they give peers will be used constructively rather than weaponized, they invest more in those relationships.

The corollary: when review systems are perceived as arbitrary or punitive, turnover follows, usually among the people you most want to keep. High performers have options. A review process that makes strong engineers feel evaluated rather than developed accelerates attrition exactly at the top of the distribution.

## What happens in the first year

Introducing 360 reviews into a team that hasn't had them surfaces things quickly. Not always comfortable things.

Often the first cycle reveals a mismatch between how managers perceive certain people and how peers experience them. Sometimes a high performer on paper turns out to have difficult working relationships that weren't visible. Sometimes someone quietly flying under the radar turns out to be deeply valued by everyone around them.

These discoveries are worth having even when they're awkward. The team's perception of the review system's credibility goes up the first time they see that 360 input actually changed something — either how a person was developed, how responsibilities were allocated, or how a working relationship was addressed.

The first year is also when you discover how honest your culture actually is. The quality of peer feedback is a readout on psychological safety. If every peer review is glowing and generic, that's data — it means people don't feel safe giving real feedback, which is its own problem worth understanding.

## The limits of the tool

360 reviews don't solve every performance management challenge.

They don't replace manager judgment. Peer input is one signal among several; it should inform the review, not determine it. Peers can be wrong, or influenced by interpersonal dynamics that don't reflect actual performance.

They're not a substitute for continuous feedback. An annual 360 that surfaces something that should have been addressed eight months ago is useful but not ideal. The review process works better when it's reinforcing ongoing conversations rather than surfacing surprises.

And they create process overhead. Not enormous overhead, but real. For a team that's already overwhelmed, adding a structured peer review cycle requires capacity. I think it's worth it, but it's not free.

## What held up over time

The purpose of a performance review isn't to evaluate people — it's to help them grow and to ensure the organization is allocating development investment well. That's easier to do when you're seeing more of the picture.

Better information drives better development conversations. Engineers who feel their contributions are actually being tracked and their growth taken seriously tend to stay. That's not a complicated mechanism — and it doesn't always play out cleanly — but it's been consistently true in practice.
